Lie 1:

It's the only way to scale

You are NOT Facebook

You won't have:

  1. 32,000+ teammates.
  2. Billions of users.
  3. Tons of different installations.
  4. World-wide deployment.
The problems that organizations like Facebook and Google have to deal with are astronomically hard. If you're building a new startup, your only advantage is speed. You can write code faster, ship sooner, and do more with less. Don't get fooled into using their tech stack, or else you're giving up your only method of beating them.

Solution 1

Just build the damned thing.

Prioritize Speed

  • Go as fast as you can.
  • Ignore shiny things.
  • Focus on the business problem.
  • Build what your customers want.


Solve scaling when you REACH scale.

Everything else is just noise.

The only thing that matters is how quickly you can build something that people want to use... solve a real problem... that they are willing to pay you to fix. The faster you can iterate on that, the greater your chance of success.
Facebook needs React because they're a giant team building a complex system that needs to work on billions of computers, phones, and little "Like" widgets deployed all over the world. Their problems, at scale, are not your problems. If the first thing your team reaches for is React, I question your decision-making abilities. You are training a generation of developers to be good developers for Facebook (and other companies that have drank the Kool-aid)

Solution 2

Build with anything

As engineers, our job is to solve business problems with technology. We are not being paid to write the best Tailwind classes. Business leaders don't care, and neither do your users. They just want you to solve their problems. The technology doesn't matter to anyone but you (and your team).

Pick the smallest version and iterate:

  • Your favorite language
  • Your favorite database
  • A single cheap VPS for server and database
  • Use a framework if you have no other choice


Sometimes... you don't even have to "build"

The stack doesn't matter, so dogmatic adherance is the behavior of someone stuck in a cult. What is your favorite language? What stack are you fastest with? Do you need that complexity? Does something exist that will get you close for your MVP?

What is the best tech stack for building an e-commerce site?

If your job is to launch a store, don't build a store until you're Amazon. Just pick Shopify or WooCommerce or Wix or Squarespace. Let someone else worry about the nonsense. Focus on your design, customer experience, and fulfillment.

The problem is not the language or the architecture, or the code... it's that our entire industry has lost it's freaking mind and thinks that choosing React as the default choice before you have React-level problems makes any sense. If you're building a mobile app and want to use React-Native, knock yourself out. That is a valid React problem. If you have a team of 1000 engineers all working on multiple dynamic front-ends fed by a massive data-center-sized API that needs to process millions of concurrent requests, sure, React is a great choice. But don't just pick it because everyone else did.

Solution 3

Build with Simplicity

As engineers, our job is to solve business problems with technology. We are not being paid to write the best Tailwind classes. Business leaders don't care, and neither do your users. They just want you to solve their problems. The technology doesn't matter to anyone but you (and your team).

Your job is to solve a business problem with software.

Pick your favorite language, grab a nice looking template, and just get cranking.

Outcomes

1. You will fail.

2. You will succeed.

3. You will pivot.

If you fail, no big deal. You didn't spend a lot of time or energy on this thing. If you succeed, great, keep building. If you pivot, great, keep building. If you reach a point where you need asynchronous processing, sure, add a microservice. But don't go all-in on AWS Lambdas yet. Just write a little bash script to do the heavy lifting. You don't need a message bus talking to a pipeline, you just need a script that looks for a database status and operates on a record.

Progressive Enhancement still rocks!

htmx has changed the game!

You can cut out tons of "front-end" complexity, push off the need to scale, and create a joyful user experience using a lightweight library like htmx. But Eric, isn't that just another freaking framework? You just finished talking about this for the past 20 minutes and ranting like a loon!

htmx in 1 minute

htmx exists as a library to ask:

  • Why should only <a> and <form> be able to make HTTP requests?
  • Why should only click and submit events trigger requests?
  • Why should only GET and POST methods be available?
  • Why does a request need to replace the entire screen?
htmx asks these questions
